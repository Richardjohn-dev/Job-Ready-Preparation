# Positioning v1

## Purpose

Create a practical first-pass positioning for CV, LinkedIn, outreach, and role targeting.
Keep job optionality and product optionality open.
Do not overclaim title, seniority, formal employment structure, or commercial outcomes.

## Current Best Read

- Broadest safe top-line label: `Software Engineer (.NET / Vue / Nuxt / SaaS / Integrations)`.
- Strongest practical fit: backend-leaning product/full-stack work rather than pure backend theory roles or pure frontend roles.
- Best near-term market: remote roles that value .NET, integrations, workflow automation, SaaS/product delivery, and some frontend capability.
- `Full-stack` is useful as a secondary descriptor, but should not be the only label everywhere.
- `Vue / Nuxt` should be visible in the positioning so frontend capability is clear without implying frontend specialization.
- Seniority should be framed as experienced and ownership-heavy, not as an unqualified `Senior Software Engineer` claim.

## Primary Positioning Statement

Product-oriented software engineer with strong .NET delivery experience across integration-heavy SaaS, document/data workflow, and background-job systems; backend-leaning overall, but comfortable shipping product-facing work across .NET, Vue, and Nuxt when the product needs it.

## Short Positioning Variants

- Software engineer focused on .NET, Vue/Nuxt, SaaS workflows, and external integrations.
- Backend-leaning .NET engineer who can also ship product-facing full-stack work in Vue/Nuxt.
- Product/software engineer for configurable integrations, workflow automation, and operationally visible SaaS systems.

## Target Role Families

- Software Engineer, .NET / Vue / Nuxt / SaaS / Integrations
- Backend .NET Engineer
- Full-Stack .NET Product Engineer
- Integrations / Platform Engineer
- Workflow Automation / Internal Tools Engineer

## Best-Fit Roles

Prioritize roles like:

- `Software Engineer (.NET / Vue / Nuxt / SaaS / Integrations)`
- `Backend .NET Engineer`
- `Full-Stack .NET Product Engineer`

Why these first:

- They fit the user's backend-leaning profile without pretending to be frontend-first, while still making modern Vue/Nuxt capability visible.
- They leave room to use both RDrive DataBridge and RefineBridge as proof.
- They match the strongest themes: integrations, background jobs, admin workflows, document/data movement, product delivery, and end-to-end ownership.

## Reach / Delay Roles

- FAANG-style algorithm-heavy software engineering roles.
- Pure frontend roles where frontend depth is the main evaluation axis.
- Pure DevOps/SRE roles.
- Highly distributed-systems roles where large-scale infra is the main story.
- Specialist principal/staff roles that expect strong formal team-lead or org-level architecture history.

## Market Preference Assumptions

- Remote-first is the main preference.
- Treat the initial market as a mix rather than one geography only.
- Best starting fit appears to be roles compatible with UK/Europe overlap, Australia-friendly overlap, or broader remote teams that can work with Thailand-based hours.
- Do not over-narrow by country before the target-role matrix is built.
- Keep both employment and product/consulting optionality alive.

## Snagr/RDrive Work History Framing

Safe framing:

> Long-term independent developer/contractor for Snagr/RDrive, owning integration-heavy product work across document/data workflows and external enterprise platforms.

Notes:

- Lead with the engineering ownership, not the informal/family-friend context.
- If asked about the structure, explain it plainly and briefly.
- Do not claim formal employment unless later confirmed and documented.
- Do not frame the experience apologetically just because much of it was solo.

## RDrive DataBridge Framing

RDrive DataBridge is the strongest commercial case study and should be treated as the anchor experience. The story is multi-year ownership of a configurable integration platform that pushed RDrive form/document workflows out to external systems and pulled external document libraries back into RDrive. The strongest safe themes are integration architecture, metadata-driven workflows, background processing, provider-specific API handling, operational visibility, and turning ambiguous business requests into shipped software. Confirmed implemented provider claims should stay anchored to Aconex, Azure Blob Storage, Autodesk, and Asite.

## RefineBridge Framing

RefineBridge should be positioned as the modern product/SaaS proof point rather than the main work-history anchor. It shows current-stack capability in .NET, Nuxt/Vue 3, and PostgreSQL, plus end-to-end ownership of hosted product workflows such as auth, billing, payments, webhooks, admin management, and product delivery. It also supports the broader story that the user is not only maintaining legacy/commercial systems, but is actively building modern product infrastructure that can power both a current SaaS and future reusable digital-product/framework work.

## Strengths To Lead With

- Multi-year ownership of integration-heavy commercial product work.
- Backend-leaning .NET strength with enough frontend depth to ship usable product workflows.
- Strong fit for document, data, workflow, and background-job problems.
- Experience making configurable systems instead of one-off hardcoded flows.
- Comfortable owning ambiguous requests end to end.
- Can speak to both product delivery and operational concerns.
- RefineBridge provides a modern stack bridge so the story is not stuck in older VB.NET/Vue/Vuetify-era work.

## Risks To Mitigate

- Title ambiguity: avoid relying on only `full-stack` or only `backend` until market feedback shows which converts better.
- Seniority ambiguity: avoid inflated claims while still making ownership visible.
- Solo-work interpretation: present solo ownership as evidence of responsibility, but prepare explicit collaboration and feedback-seeking stories.
- Interview readiness gap: strong experience exists, but explanation under pressure still needs reps.
- Location/timezone ambiguity: remote preference is clear, but exact regional targeting still needs calibration.

## Claims To Avoid

- Do not claim SharePoint was implemented.
- Do not claim 4Projects was implemented.
- Do not claim sales ownership.
- Do not claim marketing ownership.
- Do not claim product adoption outcomes or user-count outcomes without evidence.
- Do not describe the Snagr/RDrive arrangement as formal employee history unless confirmed.
- Do not call the user junior.
- Do not use an unqualified senior title where the evidence is really ownership-heavy solo experience with limited formal comparison.
- Do not imply pure frontend specialization.
- Do not imply deep DevOps/SRE specialization.
